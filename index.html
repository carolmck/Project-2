The Crust

The crust is the outermost layer of the Earth, and it makes up the planet's entire surface. The crust's depth ranges from about 5-70 km, although it makes up less than 1% of the entire volume of Earth.

There are two types of crust: oceanic and continental. Oceanic crust is 5-10 km thick but very dense. It is composed of iron magnesium silicate igneous rocks such as basalt. Continental crust is 30-50 km thick and composed of less dense sodium potassium aluminum silicate rocks such as granite.

The temperature of the crust increases with depth. Temperatures at the boundary of the crust and the mantle can range from 500° Celsius (900° Fahrenheit) to 1,000° Celsius (1,800° Fahrenheit).

The crust and the upper mantle make up the lithosphere, the layer of the Earth that is associated with tectonic activity.
Tectonic Activity: the interaction of huge slabs of lithosphere called tectonic plates

The Mantle

Layer 2: The Mantle

The mantle lies between the crust and Earth’s core. The mantle makes up 84% of Earth’s total volume and is about 2,900 kilometers (1,802 miles) thick.

The mantle is mainly composed of silicates, such as olivine, garnet and pyroxene. The mantle also contains magnesium oxide, as well as iron, potassium, aluminum and calcium.

Like the crust, the temperature of the mantle also increases with depth. Its temperature varies from 1000° Celsius (1832° Fahrenheit) near its boundary with the crust, to 3700° Celsius (6692° Fahrenheit) near its boundary with the core.

The lower mantle contains the asthenosphere, which lies directly below the lithosphere. Due to the layer's high temperature and pressure, the rocks found here are semi-molten. The tectonic plates of the lithosphere “float” on the viscous asthenosphere. The transfer of heat from the core and cooling effect of the crust creates convection currents in the mantle that power tectonic activity.

Fun Fact: Earth is the only planet with a continously active mantle.Venus has an active mantle, however, its crust and atmosphere do not allow it to change the planet's surface. Mars and Mercury both have solid, static interiors.

Layer 3: The Outer Core

The outer core lies between the mantle and the inner core. This layer is about 2,200 kilometers (1,367 miles) thick.

The outer core is composed of liquid iron and nickel. It has a low viscosity which makes this layer easily malleable.

This layer is very hot, between 4,500° and 5,500° Celsius (8,132° and 9,932° Fahrenheit).Because of its temperature and malleability, a great deal of convection occurs in this layer. This convection creates Earth’s magnetic field.

The Inner Core

Layer 4: The Inner Core

The inner core is the inner most layer of the Earth and has a radius of about 1,220 kilometers (758 miles).

This layer is even hotter than the outer core with a temperature of about 5,200° Celsius (9,392° Fahrenheit).

The inner core is described by scientists as a plasma that behaves as a solid. Because of the intense pressure that occurs in this layer (nearly 36 million atm) the iron in the inner core does not melt. The pressure and density are far too great to allow the iron atoms to move to a liquid state.

This layer rotates differently than the rest of the planet because it separated from the other layers by the liquid outer core. It rotates in the same direction as Earth’s the surface, but slightly faster. The inner core makes one extra rotation every 1,000 years.
